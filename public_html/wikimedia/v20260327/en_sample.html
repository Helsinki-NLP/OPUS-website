 <s id="3">An irregular galaxy is a galaxy that does not have a distinct regular shape, unlike a spiral or an elliptical galaxy.[1] The shape of an irregular galaxy is uncommon – they do not fall into any of the regular classes of the Hubble sequence, and they are often chaotic in appearance, with neither a nuclear bulge nor any trace of spiral arm structure.[2]</s>
 <s id="4">Collectively they are thought to make up about a quarter of all galaxies.</s>
 <s id="5">Some irregular galaxies were once spiral or elliptical galaxies but were deformed by disorders in gravitational pull.</s>
 <s id="6">Irregular galaxies may contain abundant[3] amounts of gas and dust.</s>
 <s id="7">This is not necessarily true for Dwarf Irregulars.[4]</s>
 <s id="8">There are three major types of irregular galaxies:[5]</s>
 <s id="9">An Irr-I galaxy (Irr I) is an irregular galaxy that features some structure but not enough to place it cleanly into the Hubble sequence.</s>
 <s id="10">Subtypes with some spiral structure are called Sm galaxies Subtypes without spiral structure are called Im galaxies.</s>
 <s id="11">An Irr-II galaxy (Irr II) is an irregular galaxy that does not appear to feature any structure that can place it into the Hubble sequence.</s>
 <s id="12">A dI-galaxy (or dIrrs) is a dwarf irregular galaxy[6] This type of galaxy is now thought to be important to understand the overall evolution of galaxies, as they tend to have a low level of metallicity and relatively high levels of gas, and are thought to be similar to the earliest galaxies that populated the Universe.</s>
 <s id="13">They may represent a local (and therefore more recent) version of the faint blue galaxies known to exist in deep field galaxy surveys.</s>
 <s id="14">Magellanic Clouds</s>
 <s id="15">The Magellanic Cloud galaxies were once classified as irregular galaxies.</s>
 <s id="16">The Large Magellanic Cloud has since been re-classified as type SBm [7] a type of barred spiral galaxy, the barred Magellanic spiral type.</s>
 <s id="17">The Small Magellanic Cloud remains classified as an irregular galaxy of type Im under current Galaxy morphological classification, although it does contain a bar structure.</s>
 <s id="18">Therefore, newer classification schemes place the SMC outside the irregular class as well.</s>

 <s id="21">The location of the Bo Hai.</s>
 <s id="22">The Bohai Sea or Bo Sea, also known as Bohai Gulf or Bo Gulf (渤海, lit.</s>
 <s id="23">"Bo Sea"), is the innermost gulf of the Yellow Sea and Korea Bay on the coast of Northeastern and North China.</s>
 <s id="24">It is approximately 78,000 km2 (30,116 sq. mi) in area and its proximity to Beijing, the capital of the People's Republic of China (PRC), makes it one of the busiest seaways in the world.</s>
 <s id="25">History</s>
 <s id="26">Until the early 20th century, Bo Hai was often called the Gulf of Chihli (直隸海灣 Zhílì Hǎiwān) or the Gulf of Pechihli or Pechili (北直隸海灣 Běizhílì Hǎiwān).</s>
 <s id="27">Zhili and Beizhili were historic provinces in the area surrounding Beijing.</s>
 <s id="28">Geography</s>
 <s id="29">The Bohai Sea is bounded by the Changshan Islands chain between the Liaodong and Shandong Peninsulas.</s>
 <s id="30">It has become one of busiest sea routes in recent times.</s>
 <s id="31">There are three major bays inside the Bohai Sea: Laizhou Bay to the south, Liaodong Bay to the north, and Bohai Bay to the west.</s>
 <s id="32">At the easternmost end of the Bohai Sea, between the southernmost end of the Liaodong Peninsula and northernmost end of the Shandong Peninsula, is the Bohai Strait (渤海海峡).</s>
 <s id="33">A few of the rivers entering the gulf include the Yellow, Hai, Liao, and Luan Rivers.</s>
 <s id="34">There are a few important oil reserves in the vicinity of the gulf, including the Shengli Field.</s>
 <s id="35">Important island groups or islands in the gulf include the Changshan Islands (长山列岛), Changxing Island (长兴岛), and Xizhong Island (西中岛).</s>
 <s id="36">The PRC provincial-level divisions that have a Bohai Sea coastline are, from the south, going clockwise: Shandong, Hebei, Tianjin, Hebei again, and Liaoning.</s>

 <s id="75">All limestone stalactites begin with a single mineral-laden drop of water.</s>
