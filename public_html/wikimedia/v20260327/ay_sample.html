 <s id="56">Mexico-Tenochtitlan (kastilla : México-Tenochtitlan, Spanish pronunciation: [ˈmexiko tenotʃˈtitlan]), commonly known as Tenochtitlan (Classical Nahuatl: Mēxihco-Tenōchtitlan [meːˈʃíʔ.ko te.noːt͡ʃ.ˈtí.t͡ɬan]) was a Mexica (city-state) located on an island in Lake Texcoco, in the Valley of Mexico.</s>
 <s id="57">Founded on June 20, 1325, it became the capital of the expanding Aztec Empire in the 15th century,[1] until it was captured by the Spanish in 1521.</s>
 <s id="58">Tenochtitlan covered an estimated 8 to 13.5 square kilometres (3.1 to 5.2 sq mi), situated on the western side of the shallow Lake Texcoco.</s>
 <s id="59">Lake Texcoco was the largest of five interconnected lakes.</s>
 <s id="60">Since it formed in an endorheic basin, Lake Texcoco was brackish.</s>
 <s id="61">During the reign of Moctezuma I, the "levee of Nezahualcoyotl" was constructed, reputedly designed by Nezahualcoyotl.</s>
 <s id="62">Estimated to be 12 to 16 kilometres (7.5 to 9.9 mi) in length, the levee was completed circa 1453.</s>
 <s id="63">The levee kept fresh spring-fed water in the waters around Tenochtitlan and kept the brackish waters beyond the dike, to the east.</s>
 <s id="64">Two double aqueducts, each more than 4 kilometres (2.5 mi) long and made of terracotta,[3] provided the city with fresh water from the springs at Chapultepec.</s>
 <s id="65">This was intended mainly for cleaning and washing.</s>
 <s id="66">For drinking, water from mountain springs was preferred.</s>
 <s id="67">Most of the population liked to bathe twice a day; Moctezuma was said to take four baths a day.</s>
 <s id="68">According to the context of Aztec culture in literature, the soap that they most likely used was the root of a plant called copalxocotl (Saponaria americana),[4] and to clean their clothes they used the root of metl (Agave americana).</s>
 <s id="69">Also, the upper classes and pregnant women washed themselves in a temazcalli, similar to a sauna bath, which is still used in the south of Mexico.</s>
 <s id="70">This was also popular in other Mesoamerican cultures.</s>
 <s id="71">When we saw so many cities and villages built in the water and other great towns on dry land we were amazed and said that it was like the enchantments (...) on account of the great towers and cues and buildings rising from the water, and all built of masonry.</s>
 <s id="72">And some of our soldiers even asked whether the things that we saw were not a dream? (...)</s>

 <s id="76">There were three main streets that crossed the city, each leading to one of the three causeways to the mainland of Tepeyac, Ixtapalpa, and Tlacopan.[6] Bernal Díaz del Castillo reported that they were wide enough for ten horses.</s>
 <s id="77">Surrounding the raised causeways were artificial floating gardens with canal waterways and gardens of plants, shrubs, and trees.[7] The calpullis were divided by channels used for transportation, with wood bridges that were removed at night.</s>
