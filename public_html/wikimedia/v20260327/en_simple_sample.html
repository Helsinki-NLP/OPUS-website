 <s id="12">A warm front is a leading edge of a warmer air mass that is advancing into a cooler air mass.[1][2]</s>
 <s id="13">Warm fronts usually have stratus and cirrus clouds, but sometimes they also have cumulus and cumulonimbus clouds.</s>
 <s id="14">Before the warm front passes, there can be rain or snow.</s>
 <s id="15">While it is passing, there is often light rain or drizzle.</s>
 <s id="16">Warm fronts move more slowly than cold fronts.[3]</s>
 <s id="17">On a weather map warm fronts are shown as red lines with red semicircles pointing in the direction that the front is moving.</s>

 <s id="25">A cold front is a meteorological word describing the movement of a cooler air mass into an area of warmer air.[1] The air with greater density moves under the less dense warmer air, lifting it, which can create a line of showers and thunderstorms, or a squall line to form when there is sufficient moisture.</s>
 <s id="26">This upward motion causes lowered pressure along the cold front.</s>
 <s id="27">On weather maps, the surface position of the cold front is marked with the symbol of a blue line of triangles or spikes pointing in the direction of its movement.</s>
 <s id="28">Cold fronts can also move up to twice as fast as warm fronts.</s>
 <s id="29">Precipitation</s>
 <s id="30">Rain, snow, hail, and other forms of precipitation can happen with cold fronts.[2]</s>
 <s id="31">Effects</s>
 <s id="32">Cold fronts can bring dry and colder air behind it.[3] Temperatures can drop to 15 °F behind cold fronts, and it can get very windy.</s>

 <s id="39">A pack hunter or social predator is a predator belonging to the animal kingdom, which has evolved to hunt its prey by working together with other members of its species.</s>
 <s id="40">Normally animals hunting in this way are closely related.</s>
 <s id="41">Except where only males normally hunt (humans and chimpanzees), all individuals in a family group will contribute to hunting.</s>
 <s id="42">The most commonly known pack hunter is the gray wolf.</s>
 <s id="43">The wolf is the closest wild relative of all breeds of domesticated dog.</s>
 <s id="44">Humans and their closest-living relatives chimpanzees are also pack hunters.</s>
 <s id="45">Other pack hunting mammals include dolphins, lions and spotted hyenas.</s>
 <s id="46">A number of avian social predators exist.</s>
 <s id="47">These include the Harris's hawk, butcherbirds, three of four kookaburra species and many helmetshrikes.</s>
 <s id="48">There are a few cold-blooded pack hunters.</s>
 <s id="49">Forexample, simple arthropods such as army ants.</s>
 <s id="50">Another example is the yellow saddle goatfish[1] Occasionally crocodiles are pack hunters.</s>
 <s id="51">Hunting in packs allows smaller predators to hunt larger prey.[2]</s>
 <s id="52">Ceratosaurs Temporal range: Jurassic–Cretaceous Scientific classification Kingdom: Animalia Class: Sauropsida Superorder: Dinosauria Order: Saurischia Suborder: Theropoda Infraorder: Ceratosauria Marsh, 1884</s>
 <s id="53">Ceratosaurs are a group of theropod dinosaurs.</s>
 <s id="54">They are defined as "all theropods sharing a more recent common ancestry with Ceratosaurus than with birds".</s>
 <s id="55">Ceratosauria includes the Upper Jurassic to Upper Cretaceous theropods Ceratosaurus, Elaphrosaurus, and Abelisaurus, found mainly in the southern hemisphere.</s>
 <s id="56">The earlies known Ceratosaur is from the Middle Jurassic of Patagonia, Eoabelisaurus.</s>
 <s id="57">This predates the oldest certainly known known member of the lineage by more than 40 million years.</s>
 <s id="58">The almost complete skeleton of Eoabelisaurus shows the earliest evolutionary stages of the distinctive abelisaurid modification of the forelimb.[1]</s>
 <s id="59">Originally, Ceratosauria included the above dinosaurs plus the Upper Triassic to Lower Jurassic Coelophysis and Dilophosaurus.</s>
 <s id="60">This implied a much earlier divergence of ceratosaurs from other theropods.</s>
 <s id="61">However, recent studies have shown that coelophysoids and dilophosaurids do not form a natural group with other ceratosaurs, and so they are excluded from this group.[1]</s>

 <s id="70">Its fossils look very similar to the ones from Baryonyx and Suchomimus.</s>

 <s id="72">Discovery and naming</s>
 <s id="73">The first specimens were found in Niger in 1973 by a French paleontologist named Philippe Taquet.</s>
 <s id="74">The name of the animal refers to the Latin word for crest, "crista", because it had a crest on its snout.</s>
 <s id="75">The species "lapparenti" is named after another French paleontologist called Albert-Félix de Lapparent.[1] The fossils discovered so far include two broken snout bones, some pieces from the upper and lower jaws, a claw, and four backbones (also called ''vertebrae'').</s>

 <s id="86">Siamosaurus (meaning 'Siamese lizard') is a carnivorous theropod dinosaur from the Early Cretaceous of Thailand.</s>
 <s id="87">It is only known from fossil teeth, so paleontologists don't know much about its size, but it might have reached a length of 9.1 meters (30 ft).</s>
 <s id="88">The teeth that were discovered looked a lot like those of Spinosaurus, so it probably ate fish.[1]</s>
 <s id="89">A study made in 2010 by Roman Amiot and his colleagues compared the teeth of spinosaurids like Baryonyx, Irritator, Siamosaurus, and Spinosaurus to other meat-eating dinosaurs.</s>
 <s id="90">They found that spinosaurids had very semiaquatic (living partly in water, and partly on land) lifestyles.</s>
 <s id="91">This means they lived in habitats like those of hippopotamuses, crocodiles, and turtles.</s>
 <s id="92">It also means they could exist at the same time and place as other large predators without competing for food.[2]</s>
