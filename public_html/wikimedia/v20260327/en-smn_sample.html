Ádahooníłígíí (Athabaskan pronunciation: [átàhòːníɬíkíː] Navajo: "occurrences in the area/current events"[2]) was a Navajo-language monthly newspaper that was published in the Southwestern United States from 1943 to 1957.[3] After the Cherokee Phoenix, operating from 1828 to 1834, it was the second regularly circulating newspaper in the United States that was written in a Native American language.
Ádahooníłígíí (anarâškielân Kuávlu tábáhtusah[1]) lâi maailm vuossâmuš navajokielâg aavis, mii almostui ohtii máánust Ovtâstum staatâi maadâviestârist iivij 1943–1944 já 1946–1957.[2] Aavisist lijjii uđđâseh, almottâsah já eres äigikyevdilis saavah, moh kuoskii navajoid.

It was the first newspaper to be published in Navajo[4] and the only one to have been written entirely in Navajo.[5][6] In April 2019, roughly 100 issues of the newspaper were digitized as a part of the University of Arizona Library's National Digital Newspaper Program and they are currently available online.[7]
Kielâtotkeekyevtis — navajo William Morgan, Sr. já ij-navajo Robert W. Young — toimâttáin aavis maaŋgâid iivijd oovtâst. Cuáŋuimáánust 2019 Arizona ollâopâttuv kirjerááju skannai puoh 103 numerijd já piejui täid almosin neetin, kost toh láá puohâi luuhâmnáálá nuuvtá.

History
Historjá
Ádahooníłígíí was published by the Navajo Agency of the Bureau of Indian Affairs in Window Rock, Arizona, from 1943 to 1957 and contributed to the standardization of Navajo orthography as it was widely distributed.[8] Until that time, the only widely available texts intended for a Navajo audience had been religious publications and parts of Diyin God Bizaad (a Navajo translation of the Bible).[5] Its first issue was published in August 1943.
Ton maŋa aavis almostui vistig vittii ovdilgo tot nuuvâi koskâpuddâsávt uđđâivemáánust 1944 tondiet ko nubbe maailmsuáđi ääigi Young seervâi Ovtâstum staatâi merâjyelgiviehân já varrij Window Rockist Phoenixân.[1] Tobbeen sun poorgâi nk. Koodisárnooh-projektist, mast navajokielâ kevttui syelikielân Kuálhismeerâ suátitoimâkuávlust. Suáđi nuuhâm maŋa Young maacâi Window Rockân já toimâtškuođij aavis vuot.
The paper was edited by Robert W. Young and William Morgan, Sr.
Veikkâ suáti nuuvâi jo čohčâmáánu 2. peeivi 1945, esken skammâmáánust 1946 aavis almostuuškuođij oppeet.[2][3] Suáđi maŋa Ádahooníłígíí vijđedui já toos pottii eŋgâlâskielâg koveteevstah.[2] Aavis almostui tääl mestâ njuolgâduslávt ive 1950 räi. Ive 1951 Young vuolgij toimâttâsâst já Morgan tooimâi mottoom ääigi uáivitoimâtteijen tassaažân R. Ethelyn Miller seervâi toimâttâsân.[4] Miller ij kuittâg pissoom kuhháá já jo ive 1952 aalgâst Leon Wallist šoodâi uáivitoimâtteijen.[4] Wall ääigi aavis almostui oppeet njuolgâduslávt suulân 4 ive. Čohčâmáánu 1956 rääjist aavis almostui jyehi nube máánu tassaaš ko ton almostittem nuuhâi lopâlávt vyesimáánust 1957. Muádi ive maŋeláá eŋgâlâskielâg Navajo Times vuáđudui.
From 1984 to 1989, Gauriloff served as the provincial artist for Sámi culture.[10][11] While in the Soviet Union, he met Ilpo Saastamoinen, who was there on a tour.[3] In 1991, Saastamoinen and Gauriloff visited the Kola Peninsula twice, collecting material, some of which was released on Gauriloff's 1992 album Kuäʹckkem suäjai vueʹlnn.[13] Between 1994 and 1997, the two travelled around the Kola Peninsula recording more music from the Sámi people there for the Kola Sámi Musical Tradition project of the University of Tromsø.[12][13]
Iivij 1984–1989 Gauriloff tooimâi sämikulttuur läänitaidârin.[1][2] Sovjetlittoost sun teividij Ilpo Saastamoinen, kii lâi tobbeen määđhist.[3] Ive 1991 Saastamoinen já Gauriloff eelijn Kuáládâhnjaargâst kuohtii. Tobbeen suoi nuuráin materiaalijd, main uási almostui Gauriloff ive 1992 albumist Kuäʹckkem suäjai vuelʹnn.[4] Iivij 1994–1997 kyevtis reissij eres soojijd Kuáládâhnjaargâst já paddij Kuáládâhnjaargâ sämmilij muusik Tromsa ollâopâttuv Kola Sámi Musical Tradition -proojeekt várás.[3][4]
From 1993 to 1999, Gauriloff sang the lead role in the opera trilogy Velho[12] in Äkäslompolo. He has also performed in many countries in Europe as well as in the United States and Japan.
Iivij 1993–1999 Gauriloff lávdástij uáiviroolist kuulmâuásásâš Velho-oopperast[1], mii tollui Äkäslompolost.
In addition to schlager and folk music, Gauriloff has sung on an album titled Finlande in 2002.[14]
Toos lasseen sun lâi lávdástâm maaŋgâin enâmijn Euroopist, Japanist já Ovtâstum staatâin.
In November 2013, the Sámi Parliament of Finland presented Gauriloff with an award on his 50th anniversary as an artist.[1][3]
Skammâmáánust 2013 Suomâ Sämitigge keigij Jaakko Gauriloffin palhâšume, ko sun lâi porgâm artistin jo 50 ihheed.[1]

Jaakko Gauriloff (born March 1939)[1] is a Skolt Sámi singer.
Jaakko Gauriloff (š. njuhčâmáánust 1939 Suonnjel, Suomâ)[1] lii nuorttâlâš lávloo.
He is regarded as the first artist to have sung popular music in the Skolt Sámi language and is said to be the first Sámi to have published an album in Finland when he published his first record at the end of the 1960s.[2] Although he is mostly known as a schlager singer, he can also sing traditional Skolt Sámi leuʹdds.[1][3]
Sun annoo vuossâmužžân artistin, kii lii lávlum pop-muusik nuorttâlâškielân. Toos lasseen lii ettum, et sun lâi vuossâmuš sämmilâš, kii lii almostittâm album Suomâ peln, ko suu vuossâmuš skiärru almostui 1960-lovo loopâst.[2] Sun lii peeggâlm časkoslavlui lávlon, mut sun lii meid čeppi leuddajeijee.[1][3]

Early life
Eellim
Gauriloff was born in the village of Suonikylä (Skolt Sami: Suõʹnnʼjel), Petsamo Province, Finland, a few months before the Winter War broke out.
Jaakko Gauriloff šoodâi Suonnjelist (nuorttâlâškielân Suõʹnnʼjel), Piäccám läänist, Suomâst, suulân ihepele ovdil ko tälvisuáti puállái.
The family had to be evacuated, and Gauriloff was put in a sledge to which a reindeer was harnessed to pull it.
Tälvisuáđi tiet nuorttâliih karttii tobbeen evakkon.[1] Nuorâ Jaakko pieijui määđhi várás kerrisân.
For some reason, the reindeer started, and the sledge and young Gauriloff flipped upside down into a snowbank.
Tubdâmettum suujâst vuájáán suorgânij nuuvt, ete kerris já Jaakko punjâsii já kirgelii muottui.
In spite of all this, Gauriloff did not even wake up, the sledge was righted, and the evacuation journey to the Oulu region could begin.
Puoh tábáhtum aašijn huolâhánnáá nuorâ Gauriloff ij ubâ čaranâm. Ohtâ jurgâlij kerris já sij jottájii.
During the truce period, the family returned home,[1] where Gauriloff listened to Olavi Virta and Tatu Pekkarinen on his grandfather's gramophone.[2] In 1944, after the Continuation War, the Suonikylä Skolts were once again evacuated.[4] This time, they were resettled in the Sevettijärvi area of Inari. Gauriloff's parents did not want to settle there, and thus house no.
Ko ráávhusopâmuš šoodâi já koskâráávhu aalgij, sij pessii maccâđ päikkikuávlusis.[2] Tobbeen Jaakko kuldâlij Olavi Virta já Tatu Pekkarisij pittáid äijihis gramofonist.[3] Ive 1944 nuorttâliih karttii oppeet evakkon, taan keerdi Oulu kuávlun.[2] Tääl suonnjeliih asâttuvvojii Čevetjäävri kuávlun, mut Gauriloff vaanhimeh iä halijdâm asâttuđ toho.
1 in Sevettijärvi was left empty.
Tondiet Čevetjäävri táálu 1 paasij kuárusin.
In 1956, they received a house in Nilijärvi, near Nellim, where they settled down.[3]
Ive 1956 Gauriloff vaanhimeh finnii táálu Njollâjäävrist Njellim alda.[4]
Gauriloff became even more interested in music in the 1950s when he listened to records by Olavi Virta and Veikko Tuomi at home.
1950-lovvoost Gauriloff perustui vala čyevtib muusikist, ko sun kuldâlij Olavi Virta já Veikko Tuomi skiäruid pääihist.
"I learned to sing all those songs, 'Hopeinen kuu' and everything", he later said.
"Máttájim lávluđ puoh taid lavluid 'Hopeinen kuu' já puoh", sun eeđâi maŋeláá.
When he heard that Olavi Virta was coming to perform in Ivalo, he went there to see him, travelling the 40 km (25 mi) on his bicycle.
Gauriloff kuulâi, ete Olavi Virta lâi puátimin Avelân lávdástiđ, já sun vuojij 40 km määđhi pyeráin konsert várás.
Having seen the concert, Gauriloff decided to become a singer, too.
Konsert maŋa Gauriloff meridij, ete sust-uv šadda lávloo já te 15-ihásâžžân sun lávluškuođij.
He started singing at the age of 15 and won a schlager competition that was held in Sodankylä in 1961,[1] which he had entered on a bet with his friends.[2][5] Three years later, he placed third in the Finnish Schlager Championships.[6]
Ive 1961 sun vuoitij Suáđigilist uárnejum časkoslavlui kišto,[1] kuus sun uásálistij, ko sun já ustevis lijjii hastâččâttâm tast.[2][3] Kuulmâ ive maŋa sun lâi kuálmadin časkoslavlui SM-kištoost.[1]
Gauriloff's success in the Finnish championships was noted by the magazine Suosikki, which ran a story on him, calling him "the James Dean of Nellim"[7] and "the world's northernmost schlager singer",[8] and his popularity went skyrocketing. He began to receive fan mail in Nellim.
Gauriloff miänástus časkoslavlui SM-kištoost merkkui Suosikki-lostâ, mii almostitij sust artikkâl. Artikkâlist sun kočodui "Njellim James Deanin"[1] já "maailm tavemus časkoslavlui lávlon".[2] Tast maŋa sust šoodâi uáli piivnoh jotelávt já sun uážuškuođij fanipoostâ Njellimân.
"It was terrible. I could no longer have my peace and quiet", he says of those times.
"Tot lâi kaavhâd, jiem uážžum innig leđe ráávhust", sun muštâl tain puudâin.
He was asked to go to Helsinki, and indeed went there, but did not like it, explaining: "I was not cut out for city life. I could not stay there and stare at those apartment buildings".[3]
Sun pivdui moonnâđ Helsigân já nuuvt sun moonâi, mut sun ij lijkkum kaavpugelimân já varrij oppeet taveláá.[3]
Back in the north, he was offered a two-week tour in Northern Finland together with Olavi Virta.[1] In 1970, Gauriloff moved to Rovaniemi and founded a band named Kaamos ('Polar Night').
Tobbeen sunjin fallui muošnu vyelgiđ lávdástemmáátkán Olavi Virtain.[1] Ive 1970 Gauriloff varrij Ruávinjaargân já vuáđudij tobbeen Kaamos-muusikjuávhu.
This is when he began to sing in Skolt Sámi.[2][4][6]
Talle sun lávluškuođij nuorttâlâškielân.[2][3][4]
In the early 1970s, Gauriloff performed on the television for the first time.
1970-lovo aalgâst Gauriloff lávdástij televisiost vuossâmuu keerdi.
He also toured with Nils-Aslak Valkeapää and Åsa Blind.
Sun meid vuolgij lávdástemmáátkán Nils-Aslak Valkeapääin já Åsa Blindáin.
The three artists released an album titled De čábba niegut runiidit in 1976.
Ive 1976 kuulmâs almostitij skiäru, mon nommâ lii De čábba niegut runiidit.
The album got mixed reviews, for example, Helsingin Sanomat did not consider the album to represent genuine Sámi music.
Veikkâ album maaŋgâs lijkkui albumân, Helsingin Sanomat -aavis kritikkár ij lijkkum, ko album muusik ij lamaš suu mielâst "olmâ" sämimuusik.
In 1981, Gauriloff recorded the world's first reggae single in Saami called "Tanja", which placed second in voting on the popular Finnish television show Levyraati.[1][9]
Ive 1981 Gauriloff paddij maailm vuossâmuu sämikielâg reggaesingle, ko sun paddij "Tanja" nuorttâlâškielân. Single pessij pivnohis syemmilâš TV-ohjelmân Levyraati, mast tot lâi nubben ohjelm jienâstmist.[1][2]
Apollo 7 (October 11 – 22, 1968) was the first crewed flight in NASA's Apollo program, and saw the resumption of human spaceflight by the agency after the fire that killed the three Apollo 1 astronauts during a launch rehearsal test on January 27, 1967.
Apollo 7 (roovvâdmáánu 11 – 22. peeivi 1968) lâi vuossâmuš almaidittum mánudâškirdem NASA Apollo-ohjelmist. Taat kirdem piejâi uđđâsist joton kirdemohjelm, mii koskâldittui 21 mánuppajan Apollo 1:ii luhottesvuođâ keežild. Taan luhottesvuođâst uđđivemáánu 27. peeivi 1967 jammii kulmâ astronaut rakkeet vyelgimtestaamij ohtâvuođâst.
The Apollo 7 crew was commanded by Walter M. Schirra, with command module pilot Donn F. Eisele and lunar module pilot R. Walter Cunningham (so designated even though Apollo 7 did not carry a Lunar Module).
Kommandmooduul kirden lâi Donn F. Eisele. Mánudâšmooduul kirden tooimâi R. Walter ("Walt") Cunningham. Missio kontroljuávhu stivrejii kirdemjođetteijeeh Glynn Lunney, Gene Kranz já Gerry Griffin.
The three astronauts were originally designated for the second crewed Apollo flight, and then as backups for Apollo 1.
Schirra, Eisele já Cunningham lijjii suunâttâllum nube almaidittum mánudâškirdem váldujuávkkun, já sij toimii Apollo 1:ii värijuávkkun.
After the Apollo 1 fire, crewed flights were suspended while the cause of the accident was investigated and improvements made to the spacecraft and safety procedures, and uncrewed test flights made.
Taan juávhust algâalgâlávt Eisele lâi jurdâččum Apollo 1:ii juávkun Roger Schaffee sajan, mut taah kyevtis lonottáin saje, tastko Eisele lâi lammim uálgátuáivis já tarbâšij čuopâdâs.
Determined to prevent a repetition of the fire, the crew spent long periods monitoring the construction of their Apollo command and service modules (CSM).
Apollo 7 kirdeeh testajii ehidist siämmáá mooduul ko main Apollo 1:ii kirdeeh parttâšuvvii iđedist.
Training continued over much of the 21-month pause that followed the Apollo 1 disaster.
Áinoo iäruttâssân lâi tot, et sist rakkeet uksâ ij lam kiddejum tego čuávuváá iiđeed.
