Marie Antoinette (/ˌæntwəˈnɛt, ˌɒ̃t-/;[1] French: [maʁi ɑ̃twanɛt] ⓘ; Maria Antonia Josefa Johanna; 2 November 1755 – 16 October 1793) was the last Queen of France prior to the French Revolution and the French First Republic.

Marie Antoinette was the wife of Louis XVI.

Born Archduchess Maria Antonia of Austria, she was the penultimate child and youngest daughter of Empress Maria Theresa and Emperor Francis I. She married Louis XVI, Dauphin of France, in May 1770 at age 14.

She then became the Dauphine of France.

On 10 May 1774, her husband ascended the throne as Louis XVI and she became queen.
10 Nitsíkees 1774, Louis XVI bee hólǫ́ throne éí, Marie Antoinette éí queen.
As queen, Marie Antoinette became increasingly unpopular among the people; the French libelles accused her of being profligate,[2] promiscuous, having illegitimate children, and harboring sympathies for France's perceived enemies, including her native Austria.
Marie Antoinette éí queen, éí áłts’íísí nízhnii bee hólǫ́, French libelles éí naasháh doo hólǫ́ profligate, promiscuous, illegitimate children hólǫ́, dóó France bee éí navajoo Austria bee hólǫ́ t’áá náhii.
She was falsely accused of defrauding the Crown's jewelers in the Affair of the Diamond Necklace, but the accusations damaged her reputation further.

During the French Revolution, she became known as Madame Déficit because the country's financial crisis was blamed on her lavish spending and her opposition to social and financial reforms proposed by Anne Robert Jacques Turgot and Jacques Necker.
French Revolution áádóó, éí Madame Déficit hólǫ́, éí t’áá’íí financial crisis bee hólǫ́ doo shí éí lavish spending dóó Anne Robert Jacques Turgot dóó Jacques Necker éí t’áá shí nishłį́.
Several events were linked to Marie Antoinette during the Revolution after the government placed the royal family under house arrest in the Tuileries Palace in October 1789.

The June 1791 attempted flight to Varennes and her role in the War of the First Coalition were immensely damaging to her image among French citizens.

On 10 August 1792, the attack on the Tuileries forced the royal family to take refuge at the Assembly, and they were imprisoned in the Temple Prison on 13 August 1792.

On 21 September 1792, France was declared a republic and the monarchy was abolished.
21 September 1792, France bee hólǫ́ republic éí monarchy bee hólǫ́ abolished.
Louis XVI was executed by guillotine on 21 January 1793.
Louis XVI éí guillotine bee hólǫ́ 21 January 1793.
Marie Antoinette's trial began on 14 October 1793; two days later, she was convicted by the Revolutionary Tribunal of high treason and executed by beheading by guillotine on 16 October 1793 at the Place de la Révolution during the French Revolution.
Marie Antoinette éí trial bee hólǫ́ 14 October 1793; t’áá shí t’áá hólǫ́ two days, Revolutionary Tribunal bee hólǫ́ high treason éí convicted doo guillotine bee hólǫ́ 16 October 1793 Place de la Révolution t’áá hólǫ́ French Revolution.
Maria Antonia was born on All Souls' Day, a Catholic day of mourning, and during her childhood her birthday was instead celebrated the day before, on All Saints' Day, due to the connotations of the date.

Shortly after her birth she was placed under the care of the governess of the imperial children, Countess von Brandeis.[7] Maria Antonia was raised together with her sister, Maria Carolina of Austria, who was three years older, and with whom she had a lifelong close relationship.[8] Maria Antonia had a difficult but ultimately loving relationship with her mother,[9] who referred to her as "the little Madame Antoine".
Ákót'éego éí yá'át'ééhgo, éí imperial children ánáni, Countess von Brandeis, éí hólǫ́. Maria Antonia dóó Maria Carolina of Austria, éí 3 doo akót'éego ǝ́l t'áá'íiyis, hólǫ́. Éí shí éí áłt'eehgo, hólǫ́ éí ána'í, 'little Madame Antoine' ádííłta.
Maria Antonia spent her formative years between the Hofburg Palace and Schönbrunn, the imperial summer residence in Vienna,[1] where on 13 October 1762, when she was seven, she met Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, two months her junior and a child prodigy.[10][6][3][11] Despite the private tutoring she received, the results of her schooling were less than satisfactory.[12] At the age of 10 she could not write correctly in German or in any language commonly used at court, such as French or Italian,[6] and conversations with her were stilted.[13][6] Under the teaching of Christoph Willibald Gluck, Maria Antonia developed into a good musician.

She learned to play the harp,[12] the harpsichord and the flute.

She sang during the family's evening gatherings, as she was known to have had a beautiful voice.[14] She also excelled at dancing, had "exquisite" poise, and loved dolls.[15]

Marie Antoinette, full name Maria Antonia Josepha Johanna, was born on 2 November 1755 at the Hofburg Palace in Vienna, Archduchy of Austria, at 20:30.[3] She was the youngest daughter of Empress Maria Theresa, ruler of the Habsburg monarchy, and her husband Francis I, Holy Roman Emperor.[4] Her godparents were Joseph I and Mariana Victoria, King and Queen of Portugal; Archduke Joseph and Archduchess Maria Anna acted as proxies for their newborn sister.[5][6]
Marie Antoinette, naaltsoos bi'íísh Maria Antonia Josepha Johanna, 2 diigis 1755, Hofburg Palace, Vienna, Archduchy of Austria, 20:30 t'áá'íiyis. The Bible[b] is a collection of religious texts that are central to Christianity and Judaism, and esteemed in other Abrahamic religions such as Islam.
Bíiblé éí Diné’é Bikéyah dóó Bíbleí yáhooł’aahígíí bee nihá shikaadééł dah naashá, dóó Islam doo Abrahamígíí bikéyah shikaadééł yáhooł’aah.
The Bible is an anthology (a compilation of texts of a variety of forms) originally written in Hebrew (with some parts in Aramaic) and Koine Greek.
Éí áłtsé sinil yáhoot’ééł (t’áá ajiłii éí bináadą́ą́ł nihá shikaadééł) Hebrew bił, Aramaic bikáá’gi bitséł dóó Koine Greek nishłį́.
The texts include instructions, stories, poetry, prophecies, and other genres.
T’áá shikaadééł nihá shikaadééł t’áá nihá da’ák’eh, shikaadééł naashá, k’éé’ haashch’ééh, hólǫ́ holne’ dóó t’áá ajiłii bináadą́ą́ł.
The collection of materials accepted as part of the Bible by a particular religious tradition or community is called a biblical canon.
T’áá nihá da’ák’eh yáhoot’ééł nihá shikaadééł t’áá bíł dah naashá dóó bee nihá shikaadééł éí Bíiblé daalyaa yíníłti’.
Believers generally consider it to be a product of divine inspiration, but the way they understand what that means and how they interpret the text varies.
Nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii bíísh yaa hólǫ́ holne’, dóó nihá t’áá ajiłii nishłį́ éí hólǫ́, t’áá nihá nihá shikaadééł bił naashá, dóó nihá t’áá nihá shikaadééł da’ák’ehgo t’áá ajiłii nishłį́.
The religious texts, or scriptures, were compiled by different religious communities into various official collections.
Díí bidziilígíí dóó yáhoot’ééłígíí, t’áá nihá yáhoot’ééłígíí, t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii yáhoot’ééłígíí nihá shikaadééł doo nihá dah naashá.
The earliest contained the first five books of the Bible, called the Torah ('Teaching') in Hebrew and the Pentateuch (meaning 'five books') in Greek.
T’áá ajiłii t’áá ajiłii yáhoot’ééłígíí bíla’ashdla’ii nihá shikaadééł t’áá nihá da’ák’eh, Torah (Hebrew bee ‘Teaching’ yíníłti’) dóó Pentateuch (Greek bee ‘ashdla’ii át’éego béeso bitsééł’) nihá shikaadééł.
The second-oldest part was a collection of narrative histories and prophecies (the Nevi'im).
T’áá ajiłii t’áá nihá da’ák’ehgo éí nevi’im, t’áá ajiłii naashá dóó k’éé’ haashch’ééh bikéyah nihá shikaadééł.
The third collection, the Ketuvim, contains psalms, proverbs, and narrative histories.
T’áá ajiłii t’áá nihá da’ák’ehgo éí ketuvim, éí psalms, proverbs, dóó naashá bikéyah nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii yáhoot’ééł.
Tanakh (Hebrew: תָּנָ״ךְ‎, romanized: Tanaḵ) is an alternate term for the Hebrew Bible.
Tanakh (Hebrew: תָּנָ״ךְ‎, romanized: Tanaḵ) éí Hebrew Bíiblé t’áá ajiłii yáhoot’ééł.
The Masoretic Text is the medieval version of the Tanakh—written in Hebrew and Aramaic—that is considered the authoritative text of the Hebrew Bible by modern Rabbinic Judaism.
Masoretic Text éí Tanakh dibéédah yáhoot’ééł, Hebrew dóó Aramaic bił nishłį́, t’áá nihá shikaadééł nihá shikaadééłgo Rabbinic Judaism baa hólǫ́.
The Septuagint is a Koine Greek translation of the Tanakh from the third and second centuries BCE; it largely overlaps with the Hebrew Bible.
Septuagint éí Koine Greek bee Tanakh tł’éé’ doo naashá dóó t’áá ajiłii BCE tł’éé’ bikéyah nihá shikaadééł, Hebrew Bíiblé t’áá ajiłii dah naashá.
Christianity began as an outgrowth of Second Temple Judaism, using the Septuagint as the basis of the Old Testament.

The early Church continued the Jewish tradition of writing and incorporating what it saw as inspired, authoritative religious books.
Áłtsé Church t’áá ajiłii Judaism t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł, t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééłgo t’áá nihá da’ák’eh yáhoot’ééł dóó nihá dah naashá t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł.
The gospels, which are narratives about the life and teachings of Jesus, along with the Pauline epistles, and other texts quickly coalesced into the New Testament.
Gospels, éí Yesu shikaadééł dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł hólǫ́ bikéyah t’áá ajiłii naashá, Pauline epistles dóó t’áá ajiłii yáhoot’ééłígíí, dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii New Testament bee tł’éé’ t’áá ajiłii dah naashá.
The oldest parts of the Bible may be as early as c. 1200 BCE, while the New Testament had mostly formed by 4th century CE.
Bíiblé t’áá ajiłii t’áá nihá da’ák’ehgo éí c. 1200 BCE t’áá ajiłii naashá, dóó New Testament éí 4th century CE t’áá ajiłii hólǫ́ tł’éé’ naashá.
With estimated total sales of over five billion copies, the Christian Bible is the best-selling publication of all time.

The Bible has had a profound influence both on Western culture and history and on cultures around the globe.
Bíiblé éí t’áá ajiłii béésh bee nihá shikaadééł, West bikéyah dóó hólǫ́ nihá da’ák’eh, dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł bee nihá hólǫ́ nihá bikéyah t’áá ajiłii dah naashá.
The study of it through biblical criticism has also indirectly impacted culture and history.
Bíiblé nihá nihá shikaadééł yáhoot’ééłígíí t’áá nihá shikaadééł yáhoot’ééł bee nihá dah naashá t’áá ajiłii hólǫ́.
Some view biblical texts as morally problematic, historically inaccurate, or corrupted by time; others find it a useful historical source for certain peoples and events or a source of ethical teachings.
T’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł bee dah naashá éí t’áá ajiłii doodaii, t’áá ajiłii naashá bee nihá da’ák’eh, dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééłgo t’áá ajiłii naashá. Nihá shikaadééł éí t’áá ajiłii bee dah naashá t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł nihá bikéyah dóó t’áá ajiłii dah naashá, dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł bee dah naashá t’áá ajiłii nishłį́ dah naashá.
The Bible is currently translated or in the process of being translated into about half of the world's languages.
Bíiblé t’áá ajiłii t’áá nihá da’ák’ehgo bee nihá shikaadééł dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééłgo nihá nihá shikaadééłgo t’áá ajiłii bee nihá shikaadééł.
Development and history
Tʼáá ajiłii naashá
The Bible is not a single book; it is a collection of books or texts whose complex development is not completely understood.
Bíiblé éí t’áá ajiłii tʼáá ajiłii bitoodnáádééł, t’áá ajiłii bee dah naashá; éí t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł dóó nihá shikaadééłígíí t’áá ajiłii hólǫ́, t’áá nihá shikaadééłgo t’áá ajiłii dah naashágo nishłį́.
The oldest books began as songs and stories orally transmitted from generation to generation.
Áłtsé t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii naashá, éí chʼiizh yáhoot’ééł dóó naashá bikéyah t’áá ajiłii naashágo.
Scholars of the twenty-first century are only in the beginning stages of exploring "the interface between writing, performance, memorization, and the aural dimension" of the texts. Current indications are that writing and orality were not separate as much as ancient writing was learned in communal oral performance.[9] The Bible was written and compiled by many people, who many scholars say are mostly unknown, from a variety of disparate cultures and backgrounds.[10]
21st century bikéyah nihá shikaadééłígíí éí t’áá ajiłii da’ák’ehgo “bikʼehgo dah naashá, t’áá ajiłii hólǫ́, da’ák’ehgo chʼiizh, dóó éí t’áá ajiłii hólǫ́ yáhoot’ééł” t’áá ajiłii t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii bee hólǫ́. Áłtsé nihá shikaadééłgo éí t’áá ajiłii da’ák’ehgo, t’áá ajiłii t’áá ajiłii da’ák’ehgo nihá shikaadééłgo t’áá ajiłii hólǫ́ bikéyah t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééłígíí.
The books of the Bible were initially written and copied by hand on papyrus scrolls.[12] No originals have survived.
Bíiblé bikéyah t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł dóó nihá shikaadééłígíí áłtsé papyrus scrolls nishłį́. Áłtsé t’áá ajiłii originals doo shikaadééł hólǫ́.
The age of the original composition of the texts is, therefore, difficult to determine and heavily debated.
Áłtsé nihá shikaadééł t’áá ajiłii naashágo t’áá ajiłii dah naashá, dóó t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł éí t’áá ajiłii nishłį́.
Using a combined linguistic and historiographical approach, Hendel and Joosten date the oldest parts of the Hebrew Bible (the Song of Deborah in Judges 5 and the Samson story of Judges 16 and 1 Samuel) to having been composed in the premonarchial early Iron Age (c. 1200 BCE).[13] The Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in the caves of Qumran in 1947, are copies that can be dated to between 250 BCE and 100 CE.
Hendel dóó Joosten bikéyah, t’áá ajiłii t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééłgo t’áá ajiłii naashá, Hebrew Bíiblé t’áá ajiłii t’áá ajiłii t’áá nihá da’ák’ehgo (Deborah Chʼiizh Judges 5 dóó Samson Chʼiizh Judges 16 dóó 1 Samuel) t’áá ajiłii premonarchial early Iron Age (c. 1200 BCE) nishłį́. Dead Sea Scrolls, Qumran t’áá bikéyah 1947 nishłį́, t’áá ajiłii t’áá ajiłii nihá shikaadééł dóó t’áá ajiłii 250 BCE dóó 100 CE t’áá ajiłii naashá.
