Haenyeo (also spelled haenyo) (Hangul: 해녀; lit. "sea women") are female divers in the South Korean province of Jeju, whose livelihood consists of harvesting a variety of mollusks, seaweed, and other sea life from the ocean. Known for their independent spirit, iron will and determination, haenyeo are representative of the semi-matriarchal family structure of Jeju.[1]: 1 [2][3]

Lhamo, bee Ache Lhamo, e la Tibet kɔɔ naŋ ŋmaa gili neŋ yiele aneŋ seɛre naŋ bibe yuoŋ gyamaa, ka o zie naŋ peɛle o samuni sɛŋ ka ba boɔla ka opera.
Lhamo, or Ache Lhamo, is a classical secular theatre of Tibet with music and dance that has been performed for centuries, whose nearest western equivalent is opera.
A deɛne ŋa maŋ taa la yeli mannoo aneŋ dɛmo kyɛ ka seɛ aneŋ yieli meŋ maŋ la bibe, a deɛŋ deɛneba maŋ pɔge la nimipɔgere parɛɛ parɛɛ.
Performances have a narrative and simple dialogue interspersed with comedy and satire; characters wear colorful masks.
A sensellɛ ŋa yi la a Indian Buddhist deme sensellɛ poɔ, a maŋ te kyaare nentegra mine aneŋ a Tibetan dabaŋkroŋ yɛlɛ.
The core stories of these theatrical plays are drawn mostly from ancient Indian Buddhist folk tales, lives of important people and historical events from Tibetan civilization.
Kyɛ a tigiri diibo, a seɛre aneŋ ba ŋmenpuori yɛlɛ na maŋ te kyaarɛɛ a Tibetan Royal Dynastic saŋa.
However the ceremonial, dance and ritual spectacles strongly reflects the Tibetan Royal Dynastic period.

Very similar traditions are found in Bhutan and other regions influenced by Tibetan Buddhism and Tibetan culture.
Lhamo taa la noba teɛroo ata mine ka neɛ zaa sage a di, ka ana la ama: Kyimulunga, Gyangara, and Chungba.[1]
There are at least three unique schools of Lhamo: Kyimulunga, Gyangara, and Chungba.[1]

By the turn of the 19th century, Ache Lhamo performances were found throughout Tibet, each major district having a permanent amateur troupe performing for the local community at various formal occasions throughout the year.

In the Tibetan language, the opera is called Al-che-lha-mo, the actor lha-mo-ba and the script khrab-gzhung Tibetan opera is considered to be one example of the crystallization of the cultural wisdom of Tibetan people for thousands of years.

Yeondeunghoe (Korean: 연등회; Hanja: 燃燈會; Korean pronunciation: [jʌnd​ɯ̽ŋɦø]) is a lantern-lighting festival in Korea celebrating the Buddha's Birthday.
A bebiri ŋa ma naŋ maŋ di a tigiri ŋa a yi a Korean Lunar calendar poɔ waa la a fourth lunar month bebie anii daare .

[6,8] The English translation of the name of the festival is written as Yeondeunghoe, Yeon Deung Hoe, or Yeondeung Hoe.
Aneŋ meŋ gba, a Bɔrfɔ kɔkɔre leɛroo meŋa ko a sɛgebie, “Yeon” “Deung” “Hoe” maŋ taa la muni parɛɛ parɛɛ aŋa, “Lotus Lantern Festival” and “lighting a lantern.” [7]
Likewise, the exact English translation of the words, “Yeon” “Deung” “Hoe” are varied with suggested meanings as, “Lotus Lantern Festival” and “lighting a lantern.” [7]
