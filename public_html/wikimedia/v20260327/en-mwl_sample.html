Duostories is a project that brings the Duolingo stories[1] to other languages such as Dutch[2] and Chinese, including auxillary languages such as Interlingue, personal conlangs and minority languages such as Asturian, Silesian and Neapolitan.
L Duostories ye un porjeto que trai las stórias de l Duolingo[1] pa outras lhénguas, cumo Houlandés i Mandarin, ancluindo lhénguas oussiliares cumo Interlingue, "conlangs" pessonais i lhénguas minoritárias cumo Sturiano, Mirandés i Neapolitano.

The project is approved by Duolingo and is run by a group of volunteers.[3]
L porjeto ye aprobado pul Duolingo i ye quemandado por ũa qudrilha de beluntairos.[2]
People who want to contribute must apply on Discord to gain permission to translate.
Pessonas que quieren cuntrebuir tenen que se aplicar ne l Discord pa cunseguir permisson pa traduzir.
The project's source code is hosted at GitHub.[1]
L código fuonte de l porjeto ye houspedado ne l Gitub.[1]

The Hino Patriótico (archaic orthography: Hymno Patriotico da Nação Portugueza; English: "Patriotic Hymn of the Portuguese Nation") was considered Portugal's first national anthem.[1] Marcos Portugal, who had remained living in Portugal, had, in 1808, initially named the piece as 'the Prince's Hymn' (D. João VI Hymn).
L Hino Patriótico (ortografie arcaica: Hymno Patriotico de la Nacion Pertueza; an anglés: "Hino Patriótico de la Nação Portugueza") fui cunsidrado l purmeiro hino nacional de Pertual.[1] Marcos Pertual, que permaneciu a residir an Pertual, dou, an 1808, einicialmente l nome de la peça cumo 'Hino de l Príncepe' (Hino D. João BI).
With this dedication, it was offered to the prince regent D. John and first performed for him in Brazil in 1809. The anthem was inspired by the cantata "La Speranza o sia l’Augurio Felice".
Cun esta dedicatória, fui ouferecido al príncepe regente D. João i tocado para el pula purmeira beç ne l Brasil an 1809.
The lyrics changed several times, altered by contemporary events, until settling into a more or less permanent form in 1821.[2]
L'hino fui anspirado na cantata "La Speranza o sia l’Augurio Felice".[2]
It was a particularly popular tune with the Portuguese troops of Wellington's Peninsular Army, where it was known by the words from the chorus Vencer ou morrer ("Win or die").
Fui ua melodie particularmente popular antre las tropas pertuesas de l Eisército Peninsular de Wellington, adonde era coincida pula letra de l refron Bencer ó morrer ("Ganhe ó morra").
Wellington's Advocate General, Seymour Larpent, noted in his diary on 9 June 1813, "The Portuguese are in the highest order, the men really look at least equal to ours, better than some … the infantry and the Caçadores in particular.
L'adbogado-giral de Wellington, Seymour Larpent, anotou ne l sou diairo de 9 de júnio de 1813: "Ls pertueses stan na mais alta orde, ls homes parécen rialmente pul menos eiguales als nuossos, melhores de l qu'alguns... l'anfantarie i ls Caçadores an particular.
The whole army marches very fresh hitherto, but the Portuguese in particular; they come in, even to the last mile, singing along the road."
To l'eisército marchas até agora mui frescas, mas ls pertueses an particular; eilhes chegan, até al radadeiro quilómetro, cantando pul camino."
"I have heard it boldly played in the teeth of the enemy by the Portuguese bands … It made all Portuguese hearts pant for the fight … and as the voices joined the music, Vencer o morir [Vencer ou morrer] was not sung without meaning."
"Oubi-lo tocado cun ousadie ne ls dientes de l'enimigo pulas bandas pertuesas... Fizo todos ls coraçones pertueses ansiáren pula luita... i a la medida que las bozes se juntában a la música, Vencer o morir [Vencer ou morrer] nun fui cantado sin sentido."
After D. Peter of Braganza became king and provided a new constitution, the song "Hymno da Carta" became more commonly used as the anthem, and the latter was officially decreed as such in 1834.[3]
Açpuis que D. Pedro de Bergança se tornou rei i stableciu ua nuoba custituiçon, la cançon "Hymno da Carta" passou a ser mais outelizada cumo hino, sendo este radadeiro ouficialmente decretado cumo tal an 1834.

The Kingdom of Portugal[3] was a monarchy in the western Iberian Peninsula and the predecessor of the modern Portuguese Republic.
L Reino de Pertual fui ua monarquie ne l'oeste de la Península Eibérica i l'antecessor de la moderna República Pertuesa.
Existing to various extents between 1139 and 1910, it was also known as the Kingdom of Portugal and the Algarves after 1415, and as the United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves between 1815 and 1822.
Eesistindo an bários graus antre 1139 i 1910, fui tamien coincido cumo Reino de Pertual i de ls Algarbes depuis de 1415, i cumo Reino Ounido de Pertual, Brasil i Algarbes antre 1815 i 1822.
The name is also often applied to the Portuguese Empire, the realm's overseas colonies.
L nome tamien ye frequentemente aplicado a l Ampério Pertués, las colónias ultramarinas de l reino.
The nucleus of the Portuguese state was the County of Portugal, established in the 9th century as part of the Reconquista, by Vímara Peres, a vassal of the King of Asturias.
L núcleo de l stado pertués era l Cundado Portucalense, criado ne l seclo IX ne l ámbito de la Recunquista, por Vímara Peres, bassalo de l Rei de las Stúrias.
The county became part of the Kingdom of León in 1097, and the Counts of Portugal established themselves as rulers of an independent kingdom in the 12th century, following the battle of São Mamede.
L cunceilho passou a fazer parte de l Reino de Lhion an 1097, i ls Cundes de Pertual stablecírun-se cumo gobernantes dun reino andependiente ne l seclo XII, na sequéncia de la batailha de São Mamede.
The kingdom was ruled by the Afonsine Dynasty until the 1383–85 Crisis, after which the monarchy passed to the House of Aviz.
L reino fui gobernado pula Dinastie Afonsina até a la crise de 1383-85, passado la qual la monarquia passou pa la Dinastie de Abis.

Barranquenho (Barranquenhu[2]) is a Romance linguistic variety spoken in the Portuguese town of Barrancos, near the Spanish border.
Barranquenho (Barranquenhu[1]) ye ua bariadade lhenguística románica falada na cidade pertuesa de Barrancos, acerca de la frunteira spanhola.
It is a mixed language, and can be considered either a variety of Portuguese (Alentejan Portuguese) heavily influenced by the Spanish dialects of neighbouring areas in Spain in Extremadura and Andalusia (especially those from Encinasola and Rosal de la Frontera),[3] or a Spanish dialect (Extremaduran / Andalusian) heavily influenced by Portuguese.
Ye ua lhéngua mista i puode ser cunsidrada ua bariadade de l pertués (pertués alanteijano) fuortemente anfluenciado puls dialetos spanholes de las árias bezinas de Spanha na Stremadura i na Andaluzia (specialmente ls de Ancinasola i Rosal de la Fruntera), ó un dialeto spanhol (Stremadura / Andaluzia) fuortemente anfluenciada pul pertués.
Barranquenho speakers maintain that they speak neither Spanish nor Portuguese but a third language altogether different.
Ls falantes de Barranquenho afirman nun falar nin spanhol nin pertués, mas ua terceira lhéngua cumpletamente defrente.
Ethnologue lists Barranquenho (as Barranquian) as a dialect of Extremaduran, perhaps because Barrancos was populated by settlers from Badajoz, a city in Extremadura, though not in an Extremaduran language speaking area.[4]
L Ethnologue lhista Barranquenho (cumo Barranquian) cumo un dialeto de la Stremadura, talbeç porque Barrancos fui poboado por quelonos de Badajoç, ua cidade de la Stremadura, ambora nun nua ária de lhéngua de la Stremadura.
The development of Barranquenho seems to be relatively recent, the variety developing no earlier than 1527 and likely by the early 1800s, unlike other minority linguistic varieties in the Iberian Peninsula, which have medieval roots.[5]
L zambolbimiento de l Barranquenho parece ser relatibamente recente, la bariadade nun se zambolbiu antes de 1527 i probabelmente ne l'ampeço de 1800, al cuntrairo d'outras bariadades lhenguísticas minoritárias na Península Eibérica, que ténen raízes mediebales.
Taipei (/ˌtaɪˈpeɪ/ ⓘ; Chinese: 臺北; pinyin: Táiběi),[1] officially Taipei City,[upper-roman 1] is the capital[lower-alpha 1] and a special municipality of Taiwan.[2][3] Located in Northern Taiwan, Taipei City is an enclave of the municipality of New Taipei City that sits about 25 km (16 mi) southwest of the northern port city of Keelung. Most of the city rests on the Taipei Basin, an ancient lakebed. The basin is bounded by the relatively narrow valleys of the Keelung and Xindian rivers, which join to form the Tamsui River along the city's western border.[4]
