In the 1890s, Joseph Edwin Washington and his wife Mary Bolling Kemp Washington, who owned the Wessyngton Plantation in Robertson County, Tennessee, commissioned Weeden to make portraits of several of their African-American servants, who had stayed to work for them as freedmen after emancipation.
Nder duuɓi 1890s, Joseph Edwin Washington e debbo maako Mary Bolling Kemp Washington, mo jogiiɗo Wessyngton Plantation e Robertson County, Tennessee, waɗi Weeden waɗugo fotooji feere nder maccuɓe maɓɓe afriknaaɓe Amerik, ɓe njooɗii ngam huuwugo ngam maɓɓe bana yimɓe ɓe keɓti hakke caggal nde ɓe ngaddani.
These works were about 5" x7" in size, and some were completed in pastels.[8] Weeden's near-sightedness was said to have contributed to her making closely detailed portraits having a "miniature like finish."[2] It is also thought that she may have worked from photographs of subjects.[8]
Ɗi kuuɗe ɗe ko 5" x7" e mawnude, e woɓɓe ɓe timmini nder pastel.[1] Weeden'en ɗon wi'a ko'e maako ɗon yi'i bana "go'o".[2] Ɗum ɗon miijo boo o waawi huuwugo diga fotooji yimɓe.[1]
Weeden also wrote poetry, and she combined both poetry and art in her four books published between 1898 and 1904.[1] Some of her poems were written in the black dialect, now known as African-American English, as she was inspired by stories and folktales told to her by her subjects when they were sitting for portraits.[2]
Weeden winndi boo gimol, o hawti e gimi e fannu nder defte mum nayi ɗi o wurtini hakkunde 1898 e 1904. Ɗe goɗɗe nder poems maako winndii e ɗemngal ɓaleejo, anndiraa jooni bana Farayse-Ameriknaajo Ingelese, ngam o ɓeydi e heewɓe e faalaaji yimɓe ɓe o wi'i mo nde ɓe njooɗi ngam fotooji.[1]

Maria Howard Weeden (July 6, 1846 – April 12, 1905), who signed her work and published as Howard Weeden, was an American artist and poet based in Huntsville, Alabama.
Maria Howard Weeden (6 Yuuli 1846 - 12 April 1905), mo sayni golle mum nden wurtini no Howard Weden, o wonnooɗoɗo tawonɗo e mawɗo Amerik mo wondi e Huntsville, Alabama.
After the American Civil War, she began to sell works she painted, which included portraits of many African-American freedmen and freedwomen.
Caggal konu lesdi Aameerika, o fuɗɗi feere kuuɗe ɗe o waɗi, ɗe hawti e fotooji yimɓe afriknaaɓe e rewɓe ɓe ɓe ngalaa hakke.
She exhibited her work in Berlin and Paris in 1895, where it was well received.
O holliti kuuɗe maako nder Berlin e Paris nder hitaande 1895, to ɗum jaɓaama.
She published four books of her poetry from 1898 to 1904, illustrated with her own art.
O holliti defte nayi nder nder deftere maako diga 1898 haa 1904, o holli e defɗe maako.
She was posthumously inducted into the Alabama Women's Hall of Fame in 1998.
O waɗii ko o waɗata nder Alabama Women's Hall of Fame e hitaande 1998.
Weeden was born July 6, 1846, in Huntsville, Alabama, six months after the death of her father, Dr. William Weeden, who had also been a prosperous planter.
Weeden jibini 6 lewru Yuli 1846, haa Huntsville, Alabama, lebbi jeetati caggal maayde baaba maako, Dr. William Weedan, mo o wonnoo boo moɗo moofta.
Her mother was his second wife, the former widow Jane (née Urquhart) Watkins.
Maama maako woni debbo maako ɗiɗawɗo, debbo mawɗo, Jane (woowo Urquhart) Watkins.
Weeden and her five older siblings were raised by their mother in the Weeden House in Huntsville.[1]
Weeden e ɓikkoy maako njoweeɗiɗo ɓeen mawɓe ɓe maama maɓɓe mawnini nder wuro Weedan nder Huntsville.[1]

Teresa Okure (aged 80) is a Nigerian Catholic nun.
Teresa Okure (jo duuɓi 80) woni mo'o katolikiyankeejo Naajeeriya.
She was the first African to become a member of the Society of the Holy Child Jesus.[1] She is a Professor in residence of the Department of Bible Theology, at the Catholic Institute of West Africa, Port Harcourt, Nigeria.[2] Okure teaches New Testament and Gender Hermeneutics, and has taught at the Institute since 1999.[3] She earned her Ph.D at Fordham University.
O woni Afriknaajo arandeejo mo laato nder Ummaatoore Ɓiɗɗo Yeesu.[1] O'o o'a mo'ol jannginoowo nder jaango teologiya Bibel, nder suudu Catholic Institute of West Africa, Port Harcourt, Naajeeriya.[2] Okure ɗon ekkitina Linnjiila e fassaruuye jinsu, e ɗon anndina nder janngirde diga 1999.[3] O heɓii jaango maako nder jaangirde Fordham.

O waɗi boo hooreejo polis nder Diiwal Ebonyi Naajeeriya nder 2015.
