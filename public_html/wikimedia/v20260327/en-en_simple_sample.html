The Minneapolis Central Library is a public library located in downtown Minneapolis, Minnesota. It is the largest library in the Hennepin County Library system.
The Minneapolis Central Library is a public library located in Minneapolis, Minnesota.
It bills itself as having "the third largest per capita public library collection of any major city in America with a collection of more than 2.4 million items—including books, DVDs, music, government documents."[1] The 353,000-square-foot (32,800 m2) building at 300 Nicollet Mall with two levels of underground parking was designed by César Pelli and opened on May 20, 2006.
It is the largest library in Minnesota, and the third largest library in America. It has a more than 2.4 million items. It contains books, DVDs, music, government documents.[1] The building oopened on May 20, 2006.
It has over 300 computers for use by the public, an 8,140-square-foot (756 m2) atrium, an 18,560-square-foot (1,724 m2) green roof planted with low-growing ground cover designed to "be sun- and drought-resistant", and a host of energy-efficiency measures.[1]
It has over 300 computers for public use. The entire library was build to be energy-efficiency and contains a Green roof.[1]

The Minneapolis Public Library and Information Center (MPLIC), opened on January 28, 1961 at 4th St. and Nicollet Ave. (the site of the current building). The new four-story glass-and-steel building replaced the old stone castle-like one, which was torn down (the space is now a parking lot).[1] The building included a library, as well as a planetarium, auditorium, and a small museum in its basement which became famous to school children for its Egyptian mummies. The new location in the then run-down "lower loop" district made the library a "cornerstone" of the 1960 redevelopment of downtown Minneapolis.[5]

Thirty years later library and community leaders again began to build support for a new, bigger library. Construction on the third and current library began (on the same location as the demolished second building) after Minneapolis voters approved a $140 million in funding to improve library services on November 7, 2000. The new building was designed by César Pelli, along with the Minneapolis firm Architectural Alliance,[6][7] and opened to the public on May 20, 2006.

At a cost of $250 per square foot, the library features a roof garden and substantial daylight. Its storage area was designed to be available to the public without staff assistance so that nearly all of the collection is accessible to the public.[3] Between the closing of the second library and the opening of the new building, most services were provided at the interim "Central Library Marquette" location, on two floors in Marquette Plaza (formerly the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis). The cost of providing the interim site while the old library was demolished and rebuilt exceeded $10 million.

Nokomis Library, formerly Nokomis Community Library, is a branch library serving the Nokomis East area of Minneapolis, Minnesota. One of 41 libraries in the Hennepin County Library system, Nokomis was designed by Buetow and Associates, Inc and opened in 1968 as a replacement for the nearby Longfellow Community Library. After being deemed crowded and outdated in 1999, the library underwent a renovation beginning in 2009 that saw it gain a number of environmentally friendly features and an expansion of 4,300 square feet (399 m2).

Nokomis was the newest branch added to the Minneapolis Public Library system in 1967; the previous one was the Linden Hills Community Library, which was completed in 1931.[2] It was built to replace the former Longfellow Community Library that had served the Nokomis East area for many years. In 1967, the City of Minneapolis had Buetow and Associates, Inc design the new library building, which was modeled after a tepee from the poem The Song of Hiawatha by Henry Wadsworth Longfellow.[3] Additionally, the library was named for Nokomis in said poem, making the branch the only library in the system to be named after a fictional character.[3] The library featured a reading loft, basement meeting room, and 13,426 square feet (1,247 m2).[4] Construction on it began in 1967 and concluded the following year at a total cost of $530,000.[2][5] The project utilized limestone produced by Mankato Kasota Stone, a local stone company quarrying in the Minnesota River Valley that had been responsible for providing stone for Minneapolis's Stone Arch Bridge.[6] The library opened in September 1968 and immediately doubled the circulation of the old Longfellow branch.[2]
At the time it was built, Nokomis was the first library added to the Minneapolis Public Library system in over 35 years; the previous library was the Linden Hills Community Library, which was completed in 1931.[2] It was built to replace the Longfellow Community Library. A decreased budget led to Nokomis losing its Saturday operating hours in 2004.[1] The Nokomis East Neighborhood Association, Thrivent Financial for Lutherans, and members of the community subsequently put funds towards keeping the library open on Saturdays until in mid-2005 when money was reallocated in the budget for continued Saturday operation.[3] Further budget woes continued to plague the Minneapolis Public Libraries as of early 2007, by which point the system had had to temporarily close three different branches in part due to a loss of local government aid.[7] A merger with the Hennepin County Libraries was approved by both systems' boards along with the Minneapolis City Council[8] and Nokomis reopened at 10 am on January 2, 2008, as a Hennepin County Library.[9] It remained the only building in the newly expanded 41-library system to be named for a fictional character.[10][11]

In 2007, the Minneapolis City Council appropriated a portion of its $1.35 billion 2008 budget for improvements to Nokomis and Northeast Community Library.[16] $5.2 million of the $7 million project came from the city; Hennepin County put forward $1.8 million for environmental additions to the library and other features.[17] The new building was designed by KKE Architects with significant input from a citizen advisory committee.[18] Ebert, Inc. was hired as the general contractor.[19] The old building's last day of operation was September 12, 2009, at which time it was reported in the Longfellow/Nokomis Messenger that the renovated library was expected to reopen sometime in the fall of the next year.[20] During the construction, patrons were encouraged to make use of nearby libraries including East Lake, Roosevelt, Washburn, and Southdale.[20]
In 2007, a $7 million was given to improve Nokomis Library and Northeast Community Library.[1] The old building was closed after September 12, 2009, and was expected to reopen sometime in the fall of the next year.[2] During the construction, people could use nearby libraries including East Lake, Roosevelt, Washburn, and Southdale.[2]
The renovation included expanding the size of the library about 4,300 square feet (399 m2)[1] to a total size of 17,340 square feet (1,611 m2).[2] A new lighting system that automatically adjusts based on the levels of light within the building was installed, along with a geothermal heating apparatus.[1] Other environmentally friendly interior features included carpeting constructed from recycled fibers and low-flow faucets.[21] A stormwater management system and native planting were utilized on the building's exterior.[19] The library once again incorporated materials from Mankato Kasota Stone, making the difference between the old and new exterior surfaces virtually indistinguishable.[1] The teen section was significantly expanded and a new ground-floor meeting room was built.[1] The children's section was also subject to improvements, gaining new nature-inspired design elements including bird light fixtures.[21] Collections were updated and expanded as well, including the purchasing of new books, DVDs, and CDs.[1] The library finally reopened on April 30, 2011, after being closed for 19 months of renovation.[1]

Pre-renovation Nokomis featured a hanging sculpture called Wind and Water Chime which hung above a fountain.[2] Classified as a stabile, a piece of artwork similar to a mobile only not intended to move, Wind and Water Chime consisted of 4,000 individual components, including small brass and phosphorus bronze rectangles strung on 600 yards (549 m) of filament lines.[22] The piece was designed and built by Don Celender, a conceptual artist who taught at Macalester College in Saint Paul, Minnesota.[23] The installation was intended to evoke the nearby Minnehaha Falls, which, like Nokomis, was referenced in Longfellow's The Song of Hiawatha.[3]

After Nokomis's closure for renovation, Wind and Water Chime was taken out of the library and stored.[24] Hennepin County projects costing more than $1 million are subject to the One Percent for Art program, wherein 1% of a project's budget is dedicated to the "selection, purchase and installation" of artwork within the space of the project.[24] At the recommendation of the Nokomis Library Public Art Selection Committee, Nokomis's $52,000 art budget was spent hiring Kristin Cheronis to polish, clean, protect, and restring the entire installation with the aim of returning it to its initial appearance.[24] It was reinstalled in Nokomis by Joel Pieper Fine Arts,[24] now hanging near the building's entrance on the library's west side in an adult reading area.[25] A ceremony celebrating the stabile's return was held on July 13, 2013.[24]
