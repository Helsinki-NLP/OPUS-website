The sign is possibly adapted from an Egyptian hieroglyph for a staff sling, which may have been the meaning of the name gimel. Another possibility is that it depicted a camel, the Semitic name for which was gamal. Barry B. Powell, a specialist in the history of writing, states "It is hard to imagine how gimel = "camel" can be derived from the picture of a camel (it may show his hump, or his head and neck!)".[2]

Of these, '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}q' was used to represent /k/ or /ɡ/ before a rounded vowel, '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}k' before '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}a', and '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}c' elsewhere.[3] During the 3rd century BC, a modified character was introduced for /ɡ/, and '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}c' itself was retained for /k/. The use of '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}c' (and its variant '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}g') replaced most usages of '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}k' and '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}q'. Hence, in the classical period and after, '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}g' was treated as the equivalent of Greek gamma, and '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}c' as the equivalent of kappa; this shows in the romanization of Greek words, as in 'ΚΑΔΜΟΣ', 'ΚΥΡΟΣ', and 'ΦΩΚΙΣ' came into Latin as '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}cadmvs', '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}cyrvs' and '.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}phocis', respectively.
