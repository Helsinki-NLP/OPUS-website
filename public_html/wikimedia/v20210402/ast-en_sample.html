El Llibru d'hores de Leonor de la Vega ye un códice con [miniaturas allumaes realizáu en pergamín pol artista flamencu Willem Vrelant nel sieglu XV.[1] Atópase calteníu na Biblioteca Nacional d'España (Vitr.24-2).
The Book of Hours of Leonor de la Vega is a codex of illuminated manuscript on vellum by Willem Vrelant.[1] It is in the National Library of Spain (Vitr.24-2).

L'autor Willem Vrelant nacíu en 1410 en Ultrech y fináu en Bruxes en 1481, foi alumnu de Jan van Eyck y tuvo activu en Flandes onde tuvo de realizar el llibru d'hores supuestamentte ente 1465 y 1470.[2]
The author Willem Vrelant Ultrech born in 1410 and died in Bruges in 1481, was a student of Jan van Eyck and was active in Flanders where the book had to make supuestamentte hours between 1465 and 1470.[2]
El manuscritu foi unviáu l'añu 1498 como regalu pol embaxador d'España en Flandes Diego Ramírez de Villaescusa al embaxador de Roma padre del poeta Garcilaso de la Vega.
The manuscript was sent 1498 as a gift by the ambassador of Spain in Flanders Diego Ramirez de Villaescusa ambassador to Rome father of the poet Garcilaso de la Vega.

T Leporis (T Lep / HD 32803 / HIP 23636)[1] ye una estrella variable de la constelación de Lepus, la llebre.
T Leporis (T Lep / HD 32803 / HIP 23636)[1] is a variable star in the constellation of Lepus, the Hare.
Visualmente a mediu grau de ε Leporis, alcuéntrase aprosimao a 500 años lluz del Sistema Solar.
It's located half a degree from ε Leporis in the sky; it's distance is approximately 500 light years from the Solar System.
De tipu espectral M6ev, ye una variable Mira —al igual que R Leporis, nesta mesma constelación— que'l so rellumu bazcuya ente magnitú aparente +7,40 y +14,30 con un periodu de 368,1 díes.
It has the spectral type M6ev, and is a Mira variable — as is R Leporis, in the same constellation — whose apparent magnitude varies between +7,40 and +14,30 with a period of 368.1 days.
La so temperatura superficial, desaxeradamente baxa pa una estrella, ye de 2800 K.[2]
Its surface temperature of 2800 K is unusually low for a star.[2]
Les variables Mira cuntar ente les mayores fábriques de molécules y polvu del Universu.
Mira variables are some of the major sources of molecules and dust in the Universe.
En cada pulsación, T Leporis espulsa materia al espaciu, perdiendo cada añu una cantidá de masa equivalente a la masa terrestre.
With each pulsation, T Leporis expels matter into space, each year losing an amount equivalent to the mass of Earth.
Imáxenes de T Leporis llograes col interferómetro Very Large Telescope del Observatoriu Européu del Sur (ESO) dexaron reparar una capa de gas y polvu qu'arrodia la estrella, que'l so diámetru ye unes 100 vegaes más grande qu'el del Sol.[3] Dada la gran distancia a la que s'atopen esta clase d'estrelles, la so diámetru angular aparente —a pesar del so enorme tamañu intrínsecu— pue ser de namái media millonésima parte de la solar.
Images of T Leporis obtained with the Very Large Telescope interferometer of the European Southern Observatory (ESO) have revealed a shell of gas and dust surrounding the star, whose diameter is some 100 times larger than that of the Sun.[1] Given the great distance at which this class of stars lie, its apparent angular diameter — despite its enormous size — is no more than a millionth of the solar apparent angular diameter.

La Badea de Plenty (maori: Te Moana-a-Toi; inglés: Bay of Plenty, topónimu que significa literalmente Badea de l'Abondancia) ye una rexón de la Isla Norte de Nueva Zelanda asitiada alredor del cuerpu d'agua del mesmu nome.
The Bay of Plenty (Māori: Te Moana-a-Toi[2]), often abbreviated to BOP, is a region in the North Island of New Zealand situated around the body of water of the same name.
La badea tien el so nome gracies a les observaciones de diversos poblaos maorí nos quales James Cook notó nes abondantes reserves d'alimentos, a diferencia de les sos observaciones anteriores na Badea de la Probeza (Poverty Bay).
The bay was named by James Cook after he noticed the abundant food supplies at several Māori villages there, in stark contrast to the earlier observations he had made in Poverty Bay.
